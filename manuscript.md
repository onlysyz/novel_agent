The coffee had gone cold again. It did that now, sat on the counter growing thin film while Maren stood at the window watching the street. She had stopped noticing the taste. The ritual mattered more—the kettle, the pour, the cup cradled in both hands like something worth holding.

Seven-fourteen. The paperboy would come in eleven minutes, throw it onto her porch where it always landed slightly off-center. Then Mrs. Aldwick across the street would emerge to retrieve her own, glancing this way, always glancing, as if Maren did something worth watching.

She drank. The coffee tasted like nothing. Like waiting.

Her shop sat two blocks east, closed on Sundays like the law required and like her father had taught her before he died. *Always respect the closed sign,* he'd said, and she'd nodded, understanding something different than he meant. The shop sold salvage and repair—old radios, watches with dead hearts, the small electronics people brought in when they couldn't afford new and wouldn't admit they wanted the old thing fixed. She was good at it. Her hands understood the logic of broken things.

These hands.

She set the cup down and watched her fingers against the ceramic. Steady. The tremor she'd carried at sixteen, that electric need to be doing something, had burned out years ago. What remained was patience. What remained was the ability to sit in a room and see every exit and measure the distance to each one without appearing to measure anything at all.

The street outside was empty. Grey light came down thin and slanted, the way it always came in October, cutting through the spaces between buildings like something looking for something.

Maren finished the coffee. Washed the cup. Dried it with a cloth that had been washing dishes for three years and had gone soft as skin.

The sound came at eight-two-three in the evening. A knock at her back door.

She was across the kitchen in three steps, back against the wall beside the frame, hand on the knob. Old architecture. Old habits. The door was solid oak, original to the house, and she'd replaced the lock twice without telling the landlord.

The knock came again. Three times. No urgency. No pattern she'd been trained to recognize as anything other than ordinary.

She opened the door.

The woman stood in the narrow alley behind her house, hands visible at her sides, wearing a coat too heavy for the weather. She was older than she'd been. Four years changed a face if you knew how to read it. The webwork of lines around her eyes. The way she held her left hand slightly curled, as if protecting it from something.

"Maren," Vera said. Salt-Hand, they used to call her. They didn't call her anything now. "You weren't answering your phone."

"I don't answer it."

"I know." Vera's gaze moved past her, into the kitchen. Looking for what? "I need to come in."

"You need to tell me why."

"The message came today." Vera's voice was flat, stripped of everything but the necessary words. "Pale Tower has Mira."

Maren's hands didn't shake. Her hands would never shake again. But something behind her sternum came loose, a small anchor she'd forgotten was holding her in place.

"Come in," she said.

The kitchen was too small for two people who understood violence. They sat across the table from each other, the scarred wood between them like a neutral zone. Vera hadn't touched the water Maren had poured her. The glass sweated onto the table and Maren watched a drop slide down the curve and pool on the wood grain.

"How long have you known?"

"Seven weeks. The first approach came in August." Vera's fingers traced the rim of the glass without lifting it. "I tried to find her first. Before telling you. That was the agreement, when I got out—I would watch for approaches, watch for the ones they send to collect. I thought I could—"

"You thought."

"I was wrong."

Maren stared at the woman across from her. She'd known Vera for twenty-three years, since the training houses, since before the names they'd been born with became irrelevant. Vera had been the steady one. The one who counted everything—deaths, days, the names of the dead. The one who still believed there might be a way to stop.

"Where is she?"

"Verathos. The facility in the eastern district, near the Flotilla docks. I've had someone on the outside watching the approaches. She's alive. She's—" Vera stopped. "They're keeping her alive. The demand came this morning. I transcribed it before they cut the feed."

"A demand."

"You know what they want." Vera's voice didn't rise. It never rose. "You know what they always want. The debt that was supposed to be paid."

The debt. The original debt. The contract Maren had signed at seventeen with blood she didn't understand, the service she'd given for twelve years until the weight had grown too heavy and she'd found a way out—a way that cost everything except the one thing they could always come back for.

Fifteen years of careful stillness. Fifteen years of pretending the ledger could be closed. Fifteen years of Mira, who didn't know, who believed her mother sold repair parts and was simply tired in ways sleep couldn't fix.

"They used her as leverage before," Maren said.

"They used you both. When you left. When you took her and ran." Vera's hand finally lifted the glass, drank, set it down. "The interest has been compounding. They waited until you thought it was paid. Until you forgot to watch."

"I never forgot."

"Then you forgot to be afraid."

The accusation landed where it was meant to. Maren felt it settle in her chest like the old weight, the accumulated mass of ended lives pressing against her ribs from the inside. She'd stopped running scenarios years ago. Stopped mapping contingencies in her sleep. She'd let herself believe the walls would hold.

"How do you want to do this?"

Vera slid a folded paper across the table. Maren didn't open it. The weight of it, the creases in the paper, told her everything the words inside would say.

"There's a window. Two days, maybe three, before they move her to somewhere less accessible. The facility has weaknesses. I've been studying the layout. There's a—"

"Vera."

"What?"

"You're doing the thing you do. Running the scenario like it's a math problem." Maren looked at her old colleague, her old friend, the woman who'd smuggled evidence of the breeding program out from under Pale Tower's nose and lost everything for it. "What do you want me to do?"

"I want you to do what you do." Vera's voice came out thin. "I want you to go in. I want you to get her out. I want you to burn the whole operation to the ground if you can, but mostly I want you to get her out."

"You know what that costs."

"You know what's already been paid."

Maren opened the paper. The demand was written in the clean hand of a clerk, clinical and bloodless. Terms of service. Duration of contract. The signatures at the bottom, in names she recognized from fifteen years ago, when she'd been someone else and the work had meant something different than it meant now.

"They want me back."

"They want you to finish what you started. The debt transfers to the next contract. Work it off, and you're clear. Both of you." Vera paused. "That's what they say. You know what it's worth."

She knew. The contracts were never finished. The debt compounded until the body gave out or the practitioner found a way to refuse. The only clean exit was the one they didn't provide.

"Show me the layout."

Vera unfolded her own papers, spreading them across the table, and for a moment they were simply two women bent over documents, old colleagues at the edge of something terrible. The light through the kitchen window went thin and golden as it caught the dust motes, and Maren watched them drift in the column of sun, suspended, waiting to fall.

"She doesn't know," Maren said. "About any of it. Who I was. What I did."

"She knows something. She's not stupid."

"She knows I'm tired. She knows I work too much and sleep too little. She knows—" Maren stopped. "She knows I never learned to be a mother right. That something in me came out wrong."

"You're here. You stayed. That's what mothers do in the stories." Vera's voice held the ghost of something that might have been humor once, in another life. "Important things happen on Tuesdays in books."

The words landed strangely, and Maren found herself almost smiling. Almost. The muscles remembered the motion even if the will had forgotten it.

"Where do we start?"

"Tomorrow night. The facility has a shift change at eleven. There's a maintenance entrance on the north side, service access for the water reclamation system. I have someone on the inside who can—"

"You have someone on the inside."

"A debt of my own. Unfinished." Vera met her eyes. "We all have unfinished debts. That's what keeps the world turning. That's what keeps us in the same rooms, doing the same things, pretending we've changed."

Maren looked at the papers spread across her kitchen table, the careful notations, the routes marked in colored ink. The work of someone who had never stopped being what they were. The work of someone who had simply found a different way to do it.

"You should sleep," Vera said. "You have a long two days ahead."

"I don't sleep well anymore."

"I know." Vera gathered her papers, folded them into the pocket of her coat. "Neither do I. Some of us get used to it. Some of us remember what we're used to and try to forget the rest."

She stood. The kitchen seemed to expand slightly in her absence, the air refilling the space she'd taken up. At the door, she paused.

"Mira asked about you once. Three years ago. One of my people heard about it—someone who knew someone who knew her. She was asking about your hands. About why you fidget all the time. Why you never sit with your back to a room."

"What did she say?"

"She said she noticed. That she'd always noticed. But she didn't want to make you feel watched." Vera's hand rested on the doorframe, on the old wood worn smooth by generations. "She's like that. She notices things and she doesn't say. I think she learned it from watching you."

The door closed. Maren stood in her kitchen with the cold coffee and the empty chairs and the light coming in thin and slanted through the window. Her hands were shaking after all. The tremor had simply moved deeper, where she couldn't see it, where it would only matter when she let it.

She thought about Mira at seventeen. The way she'd laughed at things that weren't funny, the way she'd cried at commercials and pretended she wasn't. The way she'd looked at her mother sometimes, as if trying to solve a problem she didn't have the numbers for. The way she'd learned to be careful around the silences, the way Maren's hands never stopped moving.

She thought about the contract, the signatures, the terms of service written in a clerk's clean hand. Fifteen years of careful stillness. Fifteen years of thinking the walls would hold.

The walls had never been hers. The stillness had never been safe. It had only been a pause between the work and whatever came next.

Maren turned off the light and stood in the dark of her kitchen, listening to the house settle around her. Tomorrow she would go back to the shop. She would open the door at nine, flip the sign, begin the day's work on the radio someone had brought in last week, the one with the dead capacitor in the power supply. She would use her hands, which had learned to be gentle with broken things, and she would wait.

And at eleven o'clock tomorrow night, she would do what she had always known she would do. She would go in.

She would get her daughter out.

She would burn it all down if she had to.

The cost had already been paid. The interest was simply what came due.

Outside, the street was dark now, the evening pressing in, and somewhere in the city her daughter was awake and afraid and waiting for a mother who had never been what she pretended to be. The kind of woman who ran repair shops. The kind of woman who sat still.

Maren moved through her house in the dark, her hands finding the familiar shapes of furniture, the doorframes, the edges of rooms. She moved like water through its channels. Like a blade returning to its sheath.

In the morning, the coffee would be cold again.

She would drink it anyway.The room smelled like salt and cabbage and the particular damp that came up through the floorboards in the mornings. Mira knew this smell. She had lived with it for three years now, since her mother left, and sometimes she forgot that other smells existed—the smell of bread baking, or the sharp green scent of the market herbs, or the mineral clean of rain on stone.

The other children were still sleeping. There were three of them: Brennan, who was nine and had a scar across his palm from a knife fight he'd told Mira about in exhaustive detail; Sera, who was five and wet the bed most nights even though she was supposed to be too old for it; and little Desh, who was four and didn't speak at all and had been here longer than anyone.

Mira sat up in her cot. The blanket fell away, thin and scratchy, and she was already reaching for the window before she remembered. The window was painted shut. It had been painted shut since before she came here, or maybe it had been painted shut the day she came. Mr. Greysalt had said he would fix it, but he always said things like that, and the window stayed painted shut, and the light came in thin and grey through the gap at the edges.

She counted the cracks in the ceiling. Thirty-seven. Same as yesterday.

Mr. Greysalt was not a bad man. This was something she had decided carefully, the way you decide important things, testing the words in your mouth before you let them settle into truth. He fed them, mostly. He made sure they went to the schooling sessions in the back of the fish-processing warehouse, where an old woman with missing teeth taught them letters and numbers and how to sit still. He did not hit them, which was more than Mira could say for the first placement, the one before her mother left, the one she tried very hard not to remember.

But Mr. Greysalt watched her. That was different from watching the other children. He watched her the way the cats watched mice from across the street—patient, evaluating, as if he were waiting for something to happen or for permission to act. She had learned to feel that watching like a pressure on the back of her neck, a weight that was there even when she couldn't see him.

Today, though. Today was important.

Mira reached for the seabird. It was tucked under her thin pillow, where she'd stuffed it last night, and she pulled it close without looking at it directly, as if she were afraid it might see how much she needed it. The seabird was old and matted, missing feathers in patches, one eye replaced with a button that her mother had sewn on a long time ago. Before she left. Before the leaving became the shape of everything.

"You have to say it right," Mira told the seabird, speaking low so the others wouldn't wake. "You can't just— You can't just say the whole thing at once, or it sounds like you're begging. You have to say it like it's not the most important thing in the world. Like it just— Like it just occurred to you."

The seabird did not answer. Mira adjusted her grip on it, smoothing the remaining feathers with her thumb.

"You could just stay," she said, trying it out. "You could stay, and then I wouldn't have to miss you."

She shook her head. Too much. Too much in one breath. She tried again.

"You don't have to go so far away."

Better. But still not right. The words were clumsy in her mouth, too big for the shape of them, like trying to pour the ocean into a cup.

"Maybe today," she whispered. "Maybe today I'll get it right."

The light through the window was getting stronger, which meant it was almost time to get up. Mr. Greysalt made them get up when the light changed, when the grey became slightly less grey, when there was enough to see by. He said it saved on candles.

Mira hugged the seabird tighter. She closed her eyes and pressed her face into the patch where the feathers were still soft, and she thought about her mother's hands. Her mother had hands that moved all the time, tapping against things, never still, and sometimes Mira would watch those hands and forget to be sad. It was like watching birds. You didn't think about where the birds had been or where they were going. You just watched them move.

She wondered if her mother would be at the Gutter Market today. She wondered if she would see her by accident, the way they had planned—not through Mr. Greysalt, who didn't know about the plan because the plan was secret, just through the two of them knowing the same place at the same time and showing up.

It wasn't really an accident. But it was supposed to feel like one.

"I'll get it right today," Mira told the seabird. "I have to."

Brennan woke first, as he always did. He sat up, rubbed his eyes, and looked around the room with the expression he always had—suspicious, calculating, like he was checking to see if anyone had moved his things while he slept. His things consisted of a length of rope and a piece of blue glass that he said was magic but was probably just blue glass.

"You're talking to that thing again," he said.

"Go back to sleep."

"It's creepy."

"I said go back to sleep."

Brennan made a face but lay back down, pulling his blanket over his head. Mira didn't care what he thought. He didn't have a seabird. He didn't have anyone waiting for him anywhere.

She didn't have anyone waiting either, not really. Her mother had left. But she was coming back. She had to be coming back. That was what the visits were for. The visits that happened at the Gutter Market, in the crowd, where two people could stand close enough to talk and no one would notice. The visits where her mother would sometimes touch her hair and sometimes not, where she would sometimes say things that sounded like they were meant for someone else, and Mira would nod even though she didn't understand.

Today was different, though. Today Mira had things to say.

The door opened. It was Mr. Greysalt, which was unusual—he usually sent one of the older boys to fetch them for breakfast—but there he was, filling the doorway with his bulk, his round face settling into what he probably thought was a kind expression. Mira had never been sure if he was actually kind or if he just performed it. Both, maybe. Both was worse.

"Children," he said. "Today is a special day. We're going somewhere new."

Mira felt something cold move through her stomach. She looked at the seabird, and the seabird's button eye stared back, reflecting nothing.

"Somewhere nice," Mr. Greysalt added. "A family wants to meet you, Mira. Isn't that exciting?"

The other children didn't look up. They had learned not to look up when Mr. Greysalt spoke. Mira was the only one who watched him, the only one who counted the things he didn't say.

"I don't want to meet a family," she said.

Mr. Greysalt's smile flickered. Just for a moment. Just enough for Mira to see the thing underneath it.

"Now, sweetheart, that's not how this works. You know that. Families don't come to you. You go to them."

"I don't want to."

"The cart is already waiting. Mr. Morreth is very eager to meet you."

Mr. Morreth. The name meant nothing to her, but it meant something to the room. Brennan pulled his blanket tighter. Little Desh made a sound that might have been a whimper or might have been nothing. Sera, still asleep, kicked at something in her dreams.

Mira stood up. She was still holding the seabird. She couldn't remember deciding to stand, but she was standing, and her legs felt strange, like they belonged to someone else, like they were moving without her permission.

"I'm supposed to go to the market today," she said. "The Gutter Market. I have to meet someone."

Mr. Greysalt's smile went away entirely. For a moment he looked like a different person—harder, older, someone Mira had never seen before. Then the smile came back, thinner than before, stretched over something that wasn't warmth.

"That's not happening today, sweetheart. Something's come up."

"I'm supposed to meet my mother."

The words hung in the air. Mr. Greysalt was very still, which was wrong, because he was never still. He was always moving, adjusting his rings, wiping his face, shuffling his feet. But now he was still, and he was looking at Mira with an expression she couldn't read, and the cold thing in her stomach grew bigger and bigger until it filled her whole body.

"Your mother knows," he said. "She'll understand."

"You didn't tell her. You didn't—"

"Mr. Morreth has very important business with your family. He knew about you before anyone else did. He's been waiting a very long time." Mr. Greysalt stepped forward, and Mira stepped back, and her shoulders hit the wall, and the seabird was pressed between her chest and her knees. "You were always going to go to him, sweetheart. This is just— We're just moving up the schedule."

"I want my mother."

"You'll see her soon enough. Mr. Morreth promises."

Mira knew what promises meant. She knew what they meant when adults made them, especially promises about soon, about later, about when things calmed down. Her mother had made promises like that. Her mother had said soon and later and when things calmed down, and then she had left, and the leaving was the only promise that came true.

"No," Mira said.

The word was small and sharp and it didn't change anything. Mr. Greysalt reached for her arm, and Mira tried to pull away, but he was too big and she was too small and the seabird fell to the floor as he dragged her toward the door. She didn't scream. Screaming was for children who didn't know how things worked. Screaming was noise that adults could ignore.

Instead, she watched the room as it got smaller. She watched Brennan sitting up now, watching her go, not helping. She watched Sera's sleeping face, peaceful and unaware. She watched little Desh, who still hadn't opened his eyes, whose small body was curled into itself like he was trying to disappear.

She watched the painted-shut window, the thin grey light leaking through, the cracks in the ceiling that she had counted so many times.

Thirty-seven cracks. Same as always.

Her mother would come for her. Her mother would know something was wrong when she didn't show up at the Gutter Market. Her mother would come and find Mr. Greysalt and make him explain, and then she would come and find Mira and bring her home.

Her mother knew things. Mira had always known that, even when she was too small to say it. Her mother knew how to move through rooms like water, how to watch everything and seem to watch nothing, how to make herself very small and very still when she needed to. Her mother had hands that never stopped moving, and there was something behind her mother's eyes that Mira had learned not to look at directly, the same way you didn't look directly at the sun or at the drowned places in the mud when the tide went out.

Her mother would come.

Mr. Greysalt pulled her down the hallway. The floor was cold under her bare feet. The walls were closing in, or maybe she was the one moving closer to them, and the hallway seemed longer than it had ever seemed before, stretching and stretching like it would never end.

The cart was waiting outside. Not Mr. Greysalt's usual cart, the one with the squeaky wheel and the smell of fish. This was a different cart, black-painted wood, drawn by a single grey horse that stood very still in the morning light. The stillness was wrong. Even from here, Mira could see the horse's muscles trembling, could see the whites of its eyes showing all the way around.

She didn't want to get in the cart.

She got in the cart anyway. Mr. Greysalt pushed her, gentle but firm, the way you might push someone you were trying not to hurt but weren't worried about hurting very much. The cart had a closed interior, curtains drawn, and it smelled like old smoke and something sweet, something that made Mira's head feel heavy when she breathed it in.

"Wait," she said. "My seabird. I left my seabird."

Mr. Greysalt's face appeared in the doorway. He was holding the seabird. She hadn't seen him pick it up.

"I'll keep it safe for you," he said.

"Give it back."

"You'll get it back. I promise."

He didn't sound like he meant it. He didn't sound like he was even trying to sound like he meant it. He was just going through the motions, saying the words because that was what you said when you took something from a child, and then he was closing the curtain, and the cart was moving, and the street was sliding away outside, and Mira was alone.

The cart smelled like old smoke and something sweet. Mira pressed her face against the small gap where the curtains didn't quite meet and watched the city go by. She watched the narrow vertical houses with their external staircases of black iron, stacked like books on a shelf. She watched the cobblestones worn smooth by generations. She watched the grey light coming in thin and slanted through the gaps in the buildings, and she tried to remember what her mother looked like in that light, but the memory was slippery, hard to hold, like trying to cup water in your hands.

Her mother would come. She had to believe that. She had to hold onto it the way she was holding onto the hope that she might get the words right this time, might finally say the thing she meant without it sounding like begging.

*You could just stay.*

But she wasn't going to get to say it. Not today. Maybe not ever.

The cart turned onto a wider street, and the buildings changed—taller, newer, built of glass and concrete that caught the light in ways the old buildings didn't. The shadows fell in geometric patterns that shifted as the cart moved, and Mira watched them move, watched them crawl across the cart floor like living things.

The cart stopped.

The curtain opened.

The man waiting outside was not what Mira had expected. She didn't know what she had expected—someone like Mr. Greysalt, maybe, soft and smiling and pretending—but this man was thin and sharp, with hair the color of iron that had rusted and eyes that were almost the same color. He wore a long coat that was too warm for the weather, and he stood very still, and he looked at Mira the way Mr. Greysalt had looked at her but worse, more patient, like he had all the time in the world and knew it.

"Mira," he said. "My name is Kael Morreth. I've been waiting for you for a very long time."

Mira didn't answer. She was looking past him, at the building behind him, at the tower rising up against the grey sky. It was made of pale stone, almost white, and it caught the light in a way that made it look like it was glowing from within. The Pale Tower. She had heard of it. The other children in Mr. Greysalt's arrangement whispered about it sometimes, told stories that were probably lies, about what happened to children who were taken there.

No one knew for certain what happened. That was the worst part. The not knowing.

"Your mother was supposed to bring you to me years ago," Kael Morreth said. He wasn't smiling, but his voice was warm, like he was saying something kind. "She made a choice to run instead. But things have a way of coming back around, don't they? Debts get paid. Promises get kept."

"I want my seabird."

"You'll have everything you need. Your mother made sure of that, even when she tried to hide you. She was very resourceful, your mother. Very determined." He extended his hand. It was thin and pale, with nails that were too long, and Mira did not want to take it. "Come inside. We have much to discuss."

Mira looked at his hand. She looked at the tower. She looked at Mr. Greysalt, who had followed them out of the cart and was now standing to the side, his round face unreadable, his hands empty.

"You said she knew," Mira said. "You said my mother knew."

Mr. Greysalt didn't answer. He was looking at Kael Morreth, waiting for permission to speak.

"It's all right," Kael said. "Tell her."

"She knew we were watching," Mr. Greysalt said. "She knew from the beginning. The visits to the Gutter Market, the messages—we let them happen because it kept you calm. It kept you manageable." He paused, and something moved across his face, something that might have been guilt or might have been nothing at all. "We always knew where you were, sweetheart. We were the ones who put you there."

The cold thing in Mira's stomach had spread to her whole body. She couldn't feel her hands. She couldn't feel her feet. She was standing on the cobblestones but she might as well have been standing on nothing, falling into the grey sky above, into the pale light that was everywhere and nowhere.

Her mother hadn't run. Her mother had been running toward something—toward the cart, toward the visits, toward the plan that Mira hadn't known about and would never get to know. Her mother had been trying to protect her, and it hadn't worked, and now Mira was here, in front of the Pale Tower, with a man who had been waiting for her for a very long time.

"Come inside," Kael Morreth said again. He was still holding out his hand. "There's so much I want to show you. So much your mother didn't have time to teach you."

Mira didn't take his hand. But she didn't run, either. Where would she run? Mr. Greysalt was behind her. The tower was in front of her. The city stretched out in every direction, full of people who didn't know her name, who wouldn't help even if she screamed, who would probably forget her the moment she was gone.

The seabird was gone. Her mother was gone. The words she had practiced were gone, dissolved into the grey light like they had never existed.

She followed Kael Morreth into the Pale Tower.

The door closed behind her.

Across the city, Vera Drowned stepped off a boat onto the stone docks, and the tide turned in her favor for the first time in years.

She had a long walk ahead of her. The Pale Tower was visible from here—a pale spire against the grey sky, catching the thin light like something precious. She had been there before. She had killed there before. She knew its corridors and its secrets, its blind spots and its guards, the things that had changed in four years and the things that never would.

She did not know that her daughter was already inside.

She did not know that the trap had closed before she even arrived.

She only knew that she was coming, and that nothing—not the Covenant, not Kael Morreth, not the accumulated weight of all her years of running—would stop her from walking through those doors.

The water lapped against the stones below. Somewhere in the drowned places of the city, the old foundations kept their secrets. Somewhere in the Pale Tower, her daughter was learning what her mother's absence had cost.

Vera counted the steps as she walked. Forty-seven. Forty-eight. Forty-nine.

The tower grew larger against the sky.

The tide had turned, but the current was stronger than she knew.The docks were quieter at this hour, the morning fishermen having already come and gone with their catches. Vera Drowned walked the stone path with her hands loose at her sides, though they weren't. The knives were there, strapped along her forearms beneath the linen sleeves, and the sap sat heavy in her left pocket, and the weight of them was familiar in a way nothing else was anymore.

Forty-seven steps from the dock to the first corner. She had counted them before, years ago, when the stones were new and the tide still knew how to be kind. The stones were old now. The tide had learned other things.

The Pale Tower rose against the sky like a bone left in the sun. Pale stone, pale light, pale sky—everything here bleached by salt and time and the particular cruelty of this city's light, which came in sideways and thin and never quite warm. Vera had walked under this light for twenty years. She had learned to read it the way others read weather, to see in its slant the quality of the hours ahead.

Not yet noon. The tower's shadow fell long across the market square, a grey shape that businesses had learned to avoid. No one set up stalls in the shadow of Pale Tower. No one lingered there without reason.

She had reason. That was the problem.

Forty-seven steps became fifty, became a hundred, became the steady rhythm of someone who had walked this path before and would walk it again if she lived. If she lived and Mira lived and the mathematics of it worked out the way it needed to. There was a formula for survival. She had learned it young, had refined it over decades of practice. Identify the problem. Eliminate the obstacles. Extract the target.

Simple. Clean. The kind of work that looked easy when you weren't the one doing it.

The market square opened around her, half-empty stalls and the smell of fish and old stone. A woman looked up from arranging eels in a wooden tray, looked at Vera's face, looked away. A man with a cart loaded with rope glanced at her hands, at the way she held them, and moved his cart a few inches to the left, just enough to leave a wider path between them. The small accommodations of ordinary fear. Vera had stopped noticing them years ago. They noticed her; that was enough.

The tower grew larger against the sky. Pale stone, pale light, the thin sound of the wind cutting through somewhere high above, a sound like someone calling out but not loud enough to hear.

She knew what waited inside. Kael Morreth, with his soft hands and his soft voice and his interest in bloodlines he couldn't possibly understand. The Covenant, with their ledgers and their contracts and their particular patience, the kind that came from knowing time moved differently for them than for ordinary people. The breeding program, the thing she had smuggled evidence of four years ago, the thing that had cost her everything and everyone and the small life she had tried to build in the Flotilla's shadow.

She knew what waited. That was why she had come alone.

No. That wasn't quite right.

She had come alone because there was no one else to come with. The Flotilla had given her a boat and a direction and the particular farewell of people who were glad to see you go. They had not offered to help. They had not asked what she planned to do. They knew what she was; that was enough. That was more than most people ever knew.

Forty-seven steps. Forty-eight. Forty-nine.

The tower was close now, close enough to see the individual stones, the way the pale surface had weathered over centuries into something not quite smooth, not quite rough. A surface that would be difficult to climb. A surface that had been designed to discourage exactly this kind of approach.

Vera had no intention of climbing.

She stopped at the edge of the shadow, just where the grey stone gave way to the ordinary grey light of the street. From here, she could see the door—a heavy thing of dark wood and darker hinges, designed to make people feel small. She had walked through that door before. She had walked out of it, too, which was the harder thing, the thing that most people never managed.

The door was watched. Two guards flanking it, not moving, not talking, the kind of stillness that came from training or from something worse. They weren't looking at her. They were looking at everything else—the street, the market, the people who passed too close and then passed on. They were looking for the kind of threat that Vera used to represent, back when she still wore the Crimson mark and carried the Covenant's work in her blood.

She didn't wear the mark anymore. The tattoos had faded in the four years since she had last renewed them, the teardrop behind her ear now just a shadow of its former self, surrounded by thirty-seven smaller marks that most people couldn't read. The Covenant could read them. The Covenant could read all of them, even the faded ones, even the ones that had faded because she had stopped believing in them.

The door opened.

Vera went still—not frozen, not afraid, just still in the way that water goes still before it moves. She watched the door, watched the figure that emerged, watched the way the light fell across his shoulders and his face and the soft hands he held clasped in front of him like a teacher waiting for his students to settle.

Kael Morreth smiled.

She had forgotten his smile. She had tried to forget all of it—the soft voice, the softer hands, the way he had looked at her fifteen years ago when she was still young enough to believe that she could stop. He hadn't aged well. Or he had aged exactly as well as someone who took what he needed from others was supposed to age. His face had that particular softness that came from never working with your hands, never carrying anything heavier than a pen.

"Maren," he said.

She didn't correct him. Maren Cole was a name that had existed once, in another life, in the years between her work for the Covenant and her exile to the Flotilla. She had used it sparingly. She had used it carefully. It wasn't the name he should be using, but it was a name he knew, and names were currency here, and she had learned long ago to accept what was offered.

"Kael," she said.

His smile widened. "You came."

"I came."

"I wasn't sure you would. After everything. After the way you left." He took a step closer, and Vera let him, let him close the distance between them, let him make the calculation that she was here on his terms, in his shadow, under his tower. "I thought perhaps you'd decided to stay in the Flotilla. I heard you were quite comfortable there. A little shop by the water. A daughter."

The word hung in the air between them. Daughter. He said it the way someone might say a spell, testing the weight of it, seeing what it did.

"Yes," Vera said.

"She's lovely. We've had her for two days now. She's adjusting." He tilted his head, a gesture that had always unnerved her, the way it suggested he was looking at something internal, some process or calculation he was running in real time. "She's stronger than I expected. Quieter. I suspect that's your influence."

Vera said nothing. Her hands were very still. The knives were very still. The nothing was very still.

"She misses you," Kael said. "I can tell. The way she watches the door. The way she flinches at certain sounds." He paused. "The way she doesn't eat."

"I'll take her home."

"You will." Kael's smile didn't change. That was the thing about him, the thing she had never been able to read. His face was a mask that had been worn so long it had fused with the skin beneath. "But not today. Not yet. There are things we need to discuss first. Terms. Arrangements. The kind of business that should happen somewhere more comfortable than the street."

"I'm comfortable."

"You always were. That was the problem." He gestured toward the door, the heavy wood, the dark hinges. "Please. Come inside. I've had tea prepared. The kind you like—the Flotilla blend, with the salt. I remembered."

He remembered. That was worse than if he had forgotten. That meant he had been thinking about her, planning for her, waiting for this moment with the same patience he brought to everything. The Covenant didn't forget. The Covenant didn't let go. She had known that when she left. She had known that the Flotilla was a temporary shelter, a borrowed grace, a few years of quiet before the bill came due.

She had hoped it would be longer.

"The tea," she said.

"The tea first. Then we can discuss what happens next."

Vera looked at the door. At the guards, who had gone even more still at Kael's emergence, their training recalibrating to accommodate this new variable. At the tower itself, rising pale and patient against the sky, waiting for her the way it had always been waiting, the way towers learned to wait.

She thought of Mira. Two days here. Two days in this place, learning what her mother's absence had cost, learning what the name Drowned meant to people who collected bloodlines like others collected stamps. She thought of the way Mira had looked at her last, on the docks, the seabird gone and the words dissolved and nothing left but the walk and the waiting and the small silence of a child who didn't know how to say goodbye.

She thought of the evidence she had smuggled four years ago. The documents, the testimony, the proof of what the breeding program was and what it meant and who it meant it for. She had sent copies to places that couldn't be reached, couldn't be silenced, couldn't be made to forget. She had thought that would be enough. She had thought that would change something.

The door was still open. Kael was still smiling. The guards were still watching.

"There's no clean exit," Vera said. It was not a question.

Kael's smile flickered, just for a moment. "No," he agreed. "There never was. For any of us."

Vera walked toward the door.

The shadow swallowed her whole.

---

Inside, the tower was exactly as she remembered. The same pale stone, the same thin light filtering through narrow windows. The same smell of old paper and old blood and the particular staleness of places that had been sealed too long. The same staircase, winding upward into darkness, the steps worn smooth by generations of feet that had climbed them for reasons that were never recorded.

Kael led the way. His footsteps were soft, almost silent, the walk of someone who had learned to move without being heard. Or perhaps just the walk of someone who had never needed to move quickly, never needed to run, never learned that silence was a gift you gave yourself when you couldn't be sure what waited around the corner.

Vera followed. Her footsteps were silent too, but different—shaped by different lessons, different necessities. She had learned to walk quietly because loud footsteps attracted attention and attention attracted violence and violence, once invited, was very difficult to decline. She had learned this young. She had never quite unlearned it.

The staircase ended in a hallway. Doors on either side, dark wood, brass handles tarnished by salt air. Kael stopped at one of them, turned the handle, pushed it open.

The room beyond was warm. A fire in a small hearth, a table with two chairs, a teapot steaming on a tray between them. A window overlooking the city, the grey rooftops and the grey streets and the grey water of the harbor beyond. A small bed in the corner, neatly made, a single pillow, a single blanket.

Empty. Mira wasn't here.

"She's not here," Vera said.

"Not yet." Kael gestured to the chairs. "Please. Sit. The tea will get cold."

Vera didn't sit. She stood in the doorway, looking at the room, looking at the bed, looking at the empty space where her daughter should have been. "Where is she?"

"Somewhere safe. Somewhere comfortable. Somewhere we can discuss her future without the distraction of her presence." Kael sat in one of the chairs, poured tea into two cups, lifted one to his lips and sipped. "She has options, you know. The bloodline she's from—it's remarkable, really. The abilities she's inherited. The potential." He set the cup down. "We'd like to cultivate that potential. With your permission, of course."

Vera's hands were very still. "No."

"I was afraid you'd say that." Kael's smile faded, not into anger but into something more patient, more terrible. "That's why we brought her here. Not to threaten. To negotiate. You have something we want—your knowledge of the Flotilla's smuggling routes, the names you kept when you left us, the evidence you distributed four years ago. And we have something you want."

Mira. He meant Mira.

"The routes are gone. I don't remember them."

"You remember enough."

"I remember nothing useful."

"Then we'll find another way to reach an understanding." Kael sipped his tea. "Take your time. Think about what you're willing to offer. The tea is getting cold, but there's more where that came from. Literally. The Flotilla blend is quite distinctive; I had to have it sourced especially."

Vera looked at the window. At the grey city, the grey rooftops, the grey water. Somewhere in this tower, Mira was waiting. Two days here. Two days of learning what the name Drowned meant, what the Crimson Covenant meant, what it cost to have a mother who had done the things Vera had done.

She thought of the evidence she had sent out four years ago. Copies in places that couldn't be reached. She had hoped it would be enough to bring the Covenant down, to burn the breeding program to ash, to make the ledger balance in some way that felt like justice. It hadn't been enough. Nothing was ever enough. The Covenant had survived worse than leaked documents and distant testimony. The Covenant would survive this too.

But maybe she could survive it differently. Maybe she could stop running and start something else.

"The evidence," she said.

Kael looked up.

"The evidence I distributed. The proof of the breeding program. The testimony from the families." She met his eyes. "I sent copies to places you can't reach. Archives. Libraries. Places where people keep records and keep asking questions. You know this."

"We know."

"You've known for four years."

"We have."

"Why wait?"

Kael set down his cup. For the first time, his smile faded into something else—not anger, not frustration, but something older. Something that looked almost like respect. "Because we wanted to see what you'd do with it. Whether you'd run. Whether you'd hide. Whether you'd come to us eventually, the way you're coming now." He paused. "You've been running for fifteen years, Maren. Vera. Whatever name you're wearing today. We've been curious whether you'd ever stop."

"And now you know."

"Now we know." He gestured to the empty chair. "Sit. Drink your tea. Tell me what you're willing to offer, and we'll tell you what we're willing to accept. It's a simple calculation. You've always understood simple calculations."

Vera sat. She picked up the cup. The tea was warm, faintly salty, exactly the blend she remembered from the Flotilla's small shop by the water. She thought of Mira, somewhere in this tower, drinking different tea, learning different lessons, waiting for a mother who might not come.

She thought of the evidence, the copies, the distant places. She thought of the names she had kept, the routes she had memorized, the small arsenal of secrets she had carried out of the Covenant fifteen years ago. She had thought they were worthless. She had thought they were leverage. She had thought they were a shield.

They were a weapon. They had always been a weapon. She had just been pointing it in the wrong direction.

"The names," she said.

Kael's eyes sharpened. "Yes?"

"The routes. The evidence. All of it." She set down the cup. "In exchange for Mira. Tonight. Before the sun sets."

"That seems—"

"It's all I have. It's all I'll give. Take it or don't."

The fire crackled. The tea cooled. Outside, the wind cut through the high windows, a sound like someone calling out but not loud enough to hear.

Kael Morreth looked at her for a long moment. Then he smiled—not the soft smile of before, but something older. Something that recognized the shape of a deal being struck, the way it always happened, the way it had happened a thousand times before with a thousand other people who had stood in this same room and made this same calculation.

"We'll need a few hours," he said. "To verify. To prepare."

"Then verify. Prepare."

"And you'll wait here. In this room. Until we're ready."

Vera looked at the bed in the corner. The single pillow. The single blanket. She thought of Mira, two days here, sleeping in a room like this one, waiting for a mother who had finally come.

"I'll wait," she said.

Kael stood. He smoothed his coat, adjusted his cuffs, performed the small rituals of a man who had never needed to rush. "The tea is quite good, isn't it? I thought you'd appreciate the gesture. The remembering."

Vera said nothing.

He left. The door closed behind him. The lock engaged—a soft sound, a heavy sound, the sound of a decision being made.

She sat in the chair by the fire and listened to his footsteps fade down the hallway. She counted them. Forty-seven. Forty-eight. Forty-nine. Then they were gone, and she was alone, and the room was very quiet except for the fire and the wind and the small percussion of her own heart beating against her ribs.

Somewhere in this tower, Mira was waiting.

Somewhere in the drowned places of the city, the old foundations kept their secrets.

Somewhere in the grey light of Verantum, the tide was turning—not in her favor, never in her favor, but in some direction, toward some outcome, toward the moment when she would walk out of this tower with her daughter or not walk out at all.

She had made deals with the Covenant before. She had survived them. She had not always understood the survival, had not always been able to explain it, but she had learned that survival was its own kind of calculation, its own kind of math. You did what you had to do. You paid what you had to pay. You walked out the door or you didn't, and if you didn't, the story ended, and if you did, the story continued, and either way, the ledger moved, and the weight accumulated, and the hands stayed exactly as dirty as they had always been.

She picked up the tea. She drank.

The Flotilla blend. The salt. The small comfort of a taste she had almost forgotten.

Somewhere below, in the grey streets of the city, the tide was turning. Somewhere above, in the higher rooms of the tower, something was being prepared. And somewhere in the space between, in the silence that had no name, Vera Drowned sat with her cold tea and her quiet hands and the long arithmetic of what came next.

Forty-seven. Forty-eight. Forty-nine.

The tower held its breath.
