

# Chapter 1

The kettle had been on since before dawn, and the wind had died sometime around four, which meant the boats would go out. Maren watched the harbor from the kitchen window. Three vessels ready to launch, their crews moving in the grey light with the efficiency of long practice. She could hear the creak of wood, a dog barking, nothing else.

Outside, gulls circled the outer buoys where the morning's catch would accumulate. Kira would have liked this. Would have sat at this same window, mug warming her hands, and said something about the quiet. The way the word *quiet* meant something different here than it did in the city. She might have talked about the fishermen's rhythm, how they moved without speaking. Or she might have said nothing at all. Sometimes Kira said nothing at all, and that was its own kind of speaking.

Maren sat at the table with her tea. The handwriting slanted hard to the right, the way it always did when she was tired. She had written it to Kira.

Seventeen years of letters. She had counted once, and then stopped counting because the number felt like an accusation. They sat in a chest by her bed, sealed with wax, addressed and never sent. She did not tell herself that she was afraid of what Kira would say when she read them.

The letter on the table was the most recent. Three pages. She had started it this morning, before the boats went out. She had stared at that sentence for an hour before setting down the pen. She did not know how to finish it. She did not know how to explain about the debt, and the timing, and the way the Syndicate had come for her when Kira was still small enough to carry in one arm. She did not know how to explain that she had left because staying would have killed them both.

The harbor bell rang twice.

She should get to the garden. The tomatoes needed tending. The squash were coming in thick this year, and she would need to give some away before they rotted on the vine. The neighbors would take them, because the neighbors were afraid of her.

She thought about this sometimes. The way the village had accepted her six years ago, when she had arrived with money enough to buy a small cottage and a story about a husband lost at sea. Aldric Thorne had been lost, in a manner of speaking. He had been in his chambers in Veldmark when Maren came through the window, and he had been on the floor when she left through the same window, and the two facts were related. Maren had called it nothing at all, because there were no words for what she had done, and the words that existed were the wrong ones.

The chest with the letters sat at the foot of the bed, and the loose board in the corner concealed a roll of leather that she had not opened in six years.

She would go to the garden. She would pull weeds and pick tomatoes and give the squash away. She would eat dinner alone and go to bed early and wake up tomorrow and do the same thing. The days would accumulate like water in a tide pool, still and shallow and going nowhere.

She had chosen to disappear, and for six years she had been disappearing, and she had thought it was working.

The harbor bell rang a third time.

She stood at the window. The boats were out on the water now, small shapes against the grey.

Then she saw Jorren coming up the path.

Jorren was sixty years old and bowlegged and he had never once asked Maren a personal question. He came to the edge of her garden and stopped, which was unusual. He did not come to her door. He stood at the garden's edge and looked at her through the low fence, and his face had something in it that she did not like.

"Maren," he said.

She set down the tomato she had been holding. "Jorren."

"There's something in the square," he said. "You should see it."

She did not ask what. She wiped her hands on her apron and came to the gate and followed him down the path toward the harbor, and the morning light fell across her shoulders like something she had forgotten how to carry.

The board was new, pale, the letters routed rather than stenciled—someone had taken care, spent time, wanted the words to look a certain way. The words were:

TO MAREN SOLIS VANE

RETURN TO SERVICE

DELIVER VAEL VELD TO KELLHOLLOW BEFORE THE FIRST FROST

UPON COMPLETION, THE GIRL WILL BE RELEASED TO THE COAST, ALIVE AND FREE OF DEBT

Below the words was a sketch. The paper was thick, expensive, the kind the Syndicate used when they wanted something to last. The sketch was well-made, the lines clean and certain. It showed Kira's face.

Maren looked at the sketch for a long time.

The circle around it was red.

"Maren." Jorren's voice was careful. "Do you know what this means?"

She knew. She knew the language, the grammar, the syntax of threat. She had written notices like this herself, once. She had stood in village squares and city harbors and watched people read her words and go pale and silent, and she had felt nothing at all, because feeling nothing was part of the work. The Quiet Tide. That was what they called her. The one who came and went and left no mark, who did the thing and walked away, who never spoke and never wavered and never once asked if there was another way.

She looked at her daughter's face on the board. The sketch was good. Not perfect. But close. Close enough that she could see the particular way Kira's eyes set in her face, the angle of her jaw, the small scar above her left eyebrow that she had gotten when she was four, falling out of a tree that Maren had told her not to climb.

The first frost. Five weeks, maybe. Maybe less.

Kellhollow. A week's journey by fast horse. Assuming everything that had to go right went right.

Vael Veld. A name she did not recognize.

"The girl will be released." They had not written Kira's name. They had written *the girl*, which meant they did not think of her as a person, which meant they would treat her accordingly.

"Maren." Jorren was still there. She had forgotten he was there. "Is there someone I can call? Someone you need?"

"No."

Her voice was steady. She noticed this with a kind of distant surprise. She had thought she might falter, might feel something crack in the foundation of the life she had built. But there was nothing. Just the same calm water she had cultivated for six years, the still surface that reflected nothing and gave nothing away.

"I'm going back to the cottage," she said.

"Do you need anything?"

She looked at him. Jorren's face was kind, or trying to be. He did not know what she was. He knew she was a widow, that she kept to herself, that she had money enough to live on but never spent it on anything except basics. He knew she was different in some way he could not name, and he had decided not to name it, and she had been grateful for six years.

"I need you to forget you saw me looking at this," she said.

He blinked. "Maren—"

"Please."

He nodded, slowly, and turned away. She watched him go, watched his bowlegged walk carry him back toward the chandlery, and then she turned and walked home.

She sat at the table. The letter was still there. Three pages. Her handwriting slanting hard to the right.

*I am writing to tell you something I should have told you years ago.*

She had wanted to tell Kira about the debt, and the Syndicate, and the way the world worked when you were young and desperate and someone offered you money for something you did not understand. She had wanted to tell her that leaving had been the hardest thing she had ever done, and that every day since then she had woken up wondering if she had made the right choice.

She had not written any of that. She had written seventeen years of letters, and she had told Kira none of the things that mattered.

Now there was a board in the harbor square with her daughter's face on it, and a red circle around her throat, and a deadline she could not miss.

She stood up.

The chest was at the foot of the bed. She knelt on the floor and lifted the lid, and the smell rose up: old paper, old ink, the faint sweetness of the wax she used to seal each envelope. Seventeen years of letters. Now she took them out.

# Chapter 2

She left the house at half past nine. The morning had burned off its mist, leaving the air sharp and salt-bright. Maren walked the long way around the square, keeping the fountain between herself and the board. The sketch was good. Someone had worked from memory or description. Kira's jaw, the set of her brow. The red circle drawn around her throat, the professional precision of it. Five weeks, the number pinned below.

The bakery sat at the corner of Saltwork and the harbor road. The windows were fogged with heat, and the smell of bread rolled out into the street like an apology. Maren pushed through the door, and the bell above it sounded the way it always sounded—tired, familiar, asking nothing of her.

The baker's wife was behind the counter, her sleeves rolled past her elbows, flour dusting her forearms. She was younger than Maren by perhaps fifteen years, with the kind of roundness that came from eating what you made rather than from anything else. She looked up and smiled, and Maren felt the old reflex, the way her body remembered what her mind had tried to forget.

"Mrs. Vane. The usual?"

"Please."

The baker's wife turned to the rack behind her. The usual was two loaves and a portion of the cheese. Maren had been buying the same things twice a week for six years. The baker's wife had never asked why a woman living alone needed so much bread.

The bell above the door rang again. Maren did not turn to look. She kept her eyes on the baker's wife, on the flour drifting from her sleeves, on the way her fingers found the cheese without needing to search. She heard footsteps—heavy, a man's stride—and then a voice, young and rough at the edges.

"Quiet morning."

"For now," the baker's wife said.

The young man came to stand beside Maren at the counter. She could see him in her peripheral vision: broad-shouldered, worker's hands. He smelled like fish and lye soap. He was looking at her, she realized. Not at the bread, not at the baker's wife. At her.

"New here," he said. It was not a question.

"Six years."

"Six years." He nodded slowly, as if this confirmed something. "My grandfather used to talk about this place. Said he knew someone from the old days. Someone who used to work for the—" He stopped himself. The baker's wife had gone very still.

"Never mind. Forget I said anything."

Maren said nothing. The silence stretched.

"My grandfather's stories," the young man continued, his voice smaller. "He was a boat-builder. Worked the harbor his whole life. He used to say there was a woman once, back before the Severance, who could walk through a room and no one would remember she'd been there. Called her the Quiet Tide." "Granfather drank too much. You know how it is."

The baker's wife began to wrap the cheese.

"He was a good storyteller," Maren said. Her voice came out flat, clipped.

The young man looked at her longer. Then he shrugged, and the tension went out of him like air from a punctured thing, and he turned to the baker's wife and asked for whatever bread fishermen asked for. Maren did not listen. She was looking at the window, at the harbor beyond it, at the boats and the water and the gulls wheeling in the salt air. She was only watching the light move across the water.

The baker's wife handed her the wrapped loaf and the cheese. Their fingers touched briefly, and the baker's wife looked away.

"Mr. Aldwin at the stable," she said quietly. "He had a mare put down last week. She's not pretty, but she's steady. If you're looking for a horse."

"Thank you."

Maren walked out into the morning. The bell rang behind her, the same tired sound, asking nothing.

The stable was on the eastern edge of the village. Mr. Aldwin was an old man with hands like knots and a face that had been weathered into something almost geographical. He was leaning against the doorframe when Maren arrived, a piece of straw working between his teeth, his eyes half-closed against the sun.

"Mrs. Vane." He did not seem surprised to see her. "Been a while."

"You had a horse put down."

"Three weeks back. Colic. Ugly thing, the whole business." He shifted the straw. "Why, you looking for a mount?"

"I might be."

He studied her. Maren let him look. She had learned that people who had lived long enough stopped trying to read her. There was nothing there to read, or so they told themselves. Just a widow who bought too much bread and walked too straight and never talked about where she'd come from.

"There's a gray," he said. "Six years old, good bloodlines, gentle enough for a woman alone. She threw a shoe last month, but I had the smith reset it. Sound as anything."

"Can you saddle her for me? Tonight, if possible. Or tomorrow morning at latest."

"Going somewhere, are you?"

"Family matter."

He nodded slowly. "Gray'll be ready. Fifty crowns, and I'll throw in a bridle. She's not fancy, but she'll get you where you're going."

Maren counted out the money. The coins were old, worn smooth, the faces on them nearly illegible. She had been saving them for six years, not knowing what she was saving them for, and now she knew.

"CUT: 'the knowing felt like the knowing she had tried so hard not to have.'"

"Tack room's around back," Mr. Aldwin said. "If you want to have a look at her first."

The mare was in the far stall, a gray thing with a blaze down her face and eyes that watched Maren with the particular attention of an animal. She was beautiful. Maren stood at the stall door. The mare's ears swiveled forward. Her nostrils flared slightly, tasting the air.

"Easy," Maren said. The mare's ears stayed forward, but she did not step back. "I know the feeling."

She turned and walked out of the stable, past Mr. Aldwin and his weathered face and his piece of straw, the village settling into the long hours before dusk. Maren walked back toward the square, toward her house, toward the harbor road and the smell of salt and the sound of the gulls crying overhead.

She stopped at the edge of the square. The board was still there. The sketch was still good. The red circle was still precise, professional, patient.

Five weeks.

She walked past it, keeping her eyes forward, her stride even. She knew what they were thinking: kidnapping, probably, the Syndicate's work, someone rich enough or connected enough to be worth taking. They would talk about it for a few days, and then they would stop, because this was how it worked.

Maren opened her door. She crossed the room to the chest by her bed. She knelt on the floor, her bad hand resting on the lid, her good hand finding the latch. The chest smelled like cedar and dust.

Inside, the letters lay in their neat stack, each one addressed to a daughter who had never received them. She pulled one out at random—the third from the top, from the spring three years ago—and unfolded it. The handwriting was careful, deliberate.

*Kira,*

*The peas are coming in early this year. I planted them the way you showed me, in rows along the south wall where they get the most sun. I never understood why it mattered, the sun. I understand now.*

*I'm sorry I haven't visited. I'm sorry for a lot of things. I don't know how to say the rest of it, so I won't. You'll have to forgive me for that too.*

*All my love,*
*Mother*

She folded the letter and put it back. She closed the lid. She stood, her knees aching. She went to the window. The harbor was turning gold, the evening light catching the water, the boats rocking gently in their berths. Somewhere out there, on the other side of the Reach, in a city she had sworn never to return to, her daughter was waiting. Or not waiting. Or gone. She did not know. She could not know. The not-knowing was the worst part, worse than the fear, worse than the rage, worse than the guilt.

She went to the door and took her coat from the hook. The roll of blades was still in the pocket, the weight of it familiar against her hip. She pulled the coat on, adjusted the fit, checked the fall of the fabric. The blades would not show. They were designed not to show.

She opened the door and stepped out into the evening. The village was quiet now, the shops closing, the fishermen coming home with their catches and their stories. Maren walked to the harbor and stood at the edge of the dock, looking out at the water. The sun was almost touching the horizon, the light stretching long and thin across the waves.

Tomorrow she would leave. Tonight she would prepare.

She stood there until the sun went down, until the sky turned gray and then black, until the stars came out one by one and the harbor lights flickered on. She stood there, and she watched the water.

When she finally turned to go, she noticed the man standing at the corner of the fishmonger's shop. He was watching her. He had been watching her for some time, she realized—long enough that his presence had faded into the background of her attention.

He did not move when she looked at him. He did not nod, or wave, or do any of the things a normal person would do when caught in the act of watching. He simply stood there, his face half in shadow, his posture suggesting nothing at all.

Maren looked at him for three heartbeats. Then she looked away, and she walked home, and she did not look back.

There would be time for all of that later.

"There's a story about you," the assistant continued. "Billy from the chandler's says he heard it from a merchant who trades up north." She leaned forward. "He says you were married to someone important once. Someone called the Quiet Tide."

"Who's that?" Her voice came out flat.

The cliff road followed the edge of the drop for nearly a quarter mile before angling inland toward her house. She had chosen the house for this—the wall at her back, the sea on one side, the single approach.

Her left hand ached. She flexed the damaged fingers, feeling the phantom pull of scar tissue.

Three hundred yards behind her, footsteps. Measured. Even.

She did not increase her pace. She did not look back. She counted the footsteps instead, tracking the rhythm, the weight distribution.

When she reached the turn where the cliff road met the inland path, she stopped. She stood there with her back to the sea, facing the house she had built, the house with the single window facing the approach.

The footsteps stopped.

She waited.

"You should keep walking." The voice came from behind her, from the cliff road. Male. Cultured. The accent of the southern Reach, Veldmark or thereabouts. "Stopping only makes it worse."

"I know who you are."

"Do you."

"I'm choosing to stop anyway."

His face was forgettable in the way trained faces were forgettable: pleasant features, unremarkable coloring, eyes that gave nothing away. She had known men like this.

"I know who sent you."

"Tell me anyway."

"What conditions."

"A single contract. One name. Vael Veld." Carsen let the name sit there. "You've heard of him."

"I know who he is."

"Then you understand the scope of what's being asked."

Maren looked past him, toward the sea. The water was gray, the horizon lost in fog. Somewhere out there, beyond the fog, beyond the reach of the shore, was a city she had not visited in six years. A tower she had once entered through the service door, leaving with blood under her nails.

"The Quiet Tide," she said. "That's what they're calling me now."

"It's a useful designation. It confirms your identity to those who need to know. It establishes your qualifications to those who might question them."

"I retired."

"You invoked the Quiet Clause. You're aware of the limitations."

"I'm told you have imagination. Use it."

Now she understood the price had never been paid. It had only been deferred.

Inside, the rooms were cold. She had not lit the fire that morning; she had left before dawn, walking down to the village in the gray light to buy bread she did not need, to stand in a crowd of people she did not know, to hear a name she had spent six years trying to forget.

She set the envelope on the table. She did not open it.

The house had three rooms: the main space with its single window, the narrow bedroom, the smaller room she had made into a study with its desk and its shelves and the chest that holds the letters. She went to the study. She knelt on the floor beside the chest, her bad knee protesting, and she opened the lid.

The letters were tied with string. Dozens of them. Years of them. She had written to Kira every week for two years, filling page after page with words she could not say aloud, explanations she could not make, apologies that meant nothing without context. Then the letters had become monthly. Then seasonally. Then she had stopped writing altogether, because the silence between them had grown vast.

She took one letter from the chest. She unfolded it without reading it. The paper was soft with age, the ink faded to brown. She could not remember what she had written.

She put the letter back. She closed the chest. She stood, slowly, her knee cracking, and she went to the window.

The cliff road stretched out below, empty. Fog had lifted slightly; she could see the village, the smoke rising from chimneys, the dark shapes of buildings against the gray sky. Somewhere in that village, Carsen was making his report. Somewhere beyond it, beyond the hills and marshes and the long road north, Kira was waiting in a room she did not choose, surrounded by people who had taken her to leverage a mother's past.

Vael Veld. The name sat in her mind like a stone.

She knew what he was. She knew what he had done. She had followed his career in the years after her retirement, reading the news of his reforms and his speeches and his quiet, persistent pressure against the Syndicate's authority. He was simply a man who believed that power should be regulated, that contracts should be subject to oversight, that killing should require more than the right price.

She had believed that once. Before the contracts. Before the fingers. Before she had learned what it cost to live in the space between intention and action.

She touched the window glass. It was cold. Outside, a gull cried, high and sharp, and she tracked its flight as it swept out over the sea, white against the gray.

At the edge of the square, she stopped.

The baker's assistant was there—Tom, the young man with the nervous hands who sometimes saved her the bread with the seeds on top. He was stacking crates, his back to her, and he didn't see her standing there. 

She said: "Tom."

He turned. His face went through the usual sequence—surprise, recognition, a quick nervous smile.

"Mrs. Vane. I was just—" He gestured at the crates. "The delivery came early."

"Have you been here long?"

"Since dawn. Mr. Ashford likes me to open up. "

"I'm fine." Maren paused. The question was there, waiting to be asked, and she had no reason to ask it except that it was there. "That board. The one with the girl's face. Do you know who put it up?"

Tom's expression shifted. Something guarded moved behind his eyes—the look of a man who knew more than he wanted to say.

"I'm not asking you to meddle. I'm asking if you know."

He was quiet for a moment. Then, in a lower voice: "I heard some men talking last night. Traders, from up north. They said the Syndicate put it there. Said it was a message for someone."

"For who?"

Tom shook his head. "They didn't say. But they said a name. I didn't know what it meant, but—" He stopped. Looked at her, and something in his face changed. "They said 'the Quiet Tide.' Like it was someone from the stories."

The words hung in the air.

"I don't know what that means," Maren said. Her voice was steady. Her hands were not.

"No," Tom said. "Of course not." He turned back to his crates, his back suddenly rigid. "I should get back to the bread. Mr. Ashford will be wondering where I've gone."

Maren  went home.

Her left hand found the edge of her sleeve. She could feel the scar beneath the fabric. It was the price she had paid to forget, and now she would pay whatever they asked, because there was no price too high.

Not for Kira.

Never for Kira.

Maren moved along the walkway at a steady pace, one hand trailing along the guide rope. She moved like someone who belonged there, someone who had paid the toll and earned the passage. She had something else.

She had been the Quiet Tide.

*

By morning, the mist had thickened to fog. She emerged from the fens at the place she expected to find it—a wooden platform overgrown with lichen, the guide rope ending at a post driven deep into the mud. Beyond it, the road stretched toward the hills.

She stopped. Breathed.

The air here was different. Dryer. The damp of the fens fell away behind her and was replaced by the thin, clean smell of altitude. She had not noticed it last night, but she noticed it now. The change in pressure. The way her lungs expanded differently.

She was close enough now.

The road was empty until she reached the first switchback. Here, a rock overhang created a shallow shelter. She sat beneath it and ate from the provisions she had packed. She ate slowly.

The sun came through the clouds in patches, turning the hill grass from grey-green to something almost gold. She watched a hawk circle above her. The bird did not know what she was. The bird did not care.

She heard it—a faint rush beneath the road noise—and she went looking for it on hands and knees, her damaged hand leaving prints in the mud. The water was cold and fast-running, clearing over stones. She cupped her palms and drank.

When she looked up, a man was standing on the road above her.

He was not young. Mid-thirties at most, with the kind of stillness that came from training rather than temperament. He wore the grey coat of the fen-folk but his boots were Syndicate-issue, hard leather good for road work. He held no weapon that she could see, but his hands were positioned for drawing one.

"You're off the path," he said. His voice was flat, giving nothing away.

"I know."

"The fens don't like people who go off the path."

"I know that too."

He studied her. She let him. Her face was what it was—older than it had been, marked by years she could not explain to strangers. She did not look like a threat.

"You're her," he said, after a moment. "The one they're talking about in Kellhollow."

She said nothing.

"The Quiet Tide." He tested the name. "You're supposed to be dead."

"Many people are supposed to be dead."

"I heard you lost the fingers."

She held up her left hand, palm toward him. The two missing digits. The scar tissue.

He looked at it. Then he looked at her face.

"The market's thick with it," he said. "Your name's been circling for three days. Something about an old debt."

"They sent you to find me."

"They sent someone. I'm the one who found you."

She nodded. This was the part she had not been able to predict—the waiting, the uncertainty.

"What do they want?" she asked.

He shook his head. "I don't know the details. I'm just the eyes."

"What do the eyes say?"

"The eyes say you're walking toward Kellhollow alone, without papers, without a guide, without any of the things a person needs to cross the fens clean." He paused. "The eyes say you're walking back in."

She wiped her wet hands on her coat, once, absently. "Then the eyes should look somewhere else."

"That's not how it works."

"Then tell me what does."

He hesitated. This was the part he had not been trained for—the grey space where his instructions ended and reality began. He had been sent to observe, not to negotiate. But she was standing in front of him, alive when she should have been dead, walking toward a place she had no right to enter, and his training did not cover the space between the facts he had been given and the fact of her presence.

"There's a safehouse," he said. "Two miles from the market. They told me to bring you there if I found you."

"They told you."

"They told me someone might come looking. They told me to watch for a woman alone, walking from the fens. They said if I found her, I should bring her in clean."

"Clean," she repeated.

"Without drawing attention."

She considered the safehouse. Two miles from the market. A place where she could ask the questions that needed asking.

"All right," she said.

He blinked. He had expected resistance, or negotiation, or something other than the flat acceptance she gave him. He had been prepared to argue. He had not been prepared for her to agree.

"All right," she said again. "Show me."

*

The safehouse was a converted grain store on the eastern edge of the market district, three stories of old stone with windows that faced the hills. The man led her through a back entrance, along a narrow corridor that smelled of dust and old flour, up a set of wooden stairs that creaked under their weight.

The room at the top was bare except for a table, two chairs, and a lamp that someone had already lit. The light was yellow and thin, casting shadows in the corners. Maren stood in the center of the room and waited.

"You'll need to stay here," the man said. "Until they send someone."

"They'll send someone."

She sat in one of the chairs. The seat was hard but not uncomfortable. She folded her hands in her lap, her damaged one over her good one, and watched the door.

He left. She heard his footsteps descending, then a door closing somewhere below.

She studied the room. The window was narrow, set high in the wall, letting in a slanted bar of grey light. She could not see the market from here. She could hear it—the distant murmur of voices, the creak of cart wheels, the cry of a child somewhere out of sight.

Kellhollow. The last time had been for the Auction, and she had stood in the crowd with her new name and her old skills, watching the numbers rise and fall like tides.

The door opened.

The woman who entered was not what Maren expected. She was younger—late thirties, perhaps—with dark hair pulled back severely from a face that was all angles and planes. She wore the plain clothes of the market district, wool and linen in muted colors, but her posture was wrong for a market woman. Too straight. Too controlled.

"You came," she said.
